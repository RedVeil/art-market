Additional Information

Although they sold their own sculpture collection to set up the Cass Sculpture Foundation, they still own a number of pieces displayed in their house on the Goodwood estate.
He curated the exhibition ‘Iron Stone’ at Kidwelly Castle, Wales.
He decided in 2008 to commission 40 large street sculptures for the London Olympics, believing it was important to promote the arts at such a crucial time.
He started by collecting maquettes by Henry Moore and went on to open a small gallery in Knightsbridge where he showed his drawings.
His brother Eric and wife Jean Cass were also art collectors and donated more than 300 works of Modern and Contemporary art to the Contemporary Art Society, with the aim of their being allocated to seven UK institutions outside of London.
His great-uncle Paul was a gallery-owner and among the first to champion the work of Van Gogh and Cézanne.
The Cass Sculpture Foundation commissions only native artists, which have included in the past Eduardo Paolozzi, Andy Goldsworthy and Rachel Whiteread, with a focus on works of a monumental scale.
The Cass Sculpture Foundation has around 60 works on display at any one time, with prices ranging from £6,000 to almost £1 million.
The Cass Sculpture Foundation supports emerging sculptors by commissioning works, including paying for the materials and transport fees, after which the works are exhibited on the Goodwood Estate. The works are then sold and the price is divided between the artist and the foundation, with the latter's share going towards commissioning new works.
The Cass Sculpture Foundation, in collaboration with the advertizing agency BBH and billboard company JCDecaux, set up an 'Art Outside' event over four days, in which billboards were set up along Tottenham Court Road, London onto which the public was invited to create an artwork (2012).
The National Portrait Gallery, London, has a portrait of Wilfred and Jeannette Cass by the artist Anne-Katrin Purkiss in their collection (2003).
